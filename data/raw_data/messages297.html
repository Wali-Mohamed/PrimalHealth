The author- Amélie (@ cottageremedies on instagram) does great work supporting raw food and terrain theory ideas. This response is for the purpose of spurring discussion and showing different viewpoints so we can all learn.

First, terms should be clarified. “Salt” has two definitions. The technical/chemistry definition- meaning any compound of a base and acid where its charge is neutralized. Countless compounds are salts, sodium chloride is just one of many. The common/historic definition is broad and can apply to any mineral-like substance which tastes salty. These two definitions overlap in a way that can be very confusing, especially when talking about diet and chemistry of the body. For example, using the chemistry definition- “salt is an essential nutrient” is true in the sense that if we didn’t consume any salts we would absolutely be unable to survive, as salts are in every food. Though that statement is inherently flawed because “salt” refers to a chemical classification yet no particular chemical is specified, so it’s literally as accurate as saying “metal is an essential nutrient”... You’ll hear the phrase “salt is an essential nutrient” repeated all over the internet among those who study health, mistaken as referring to “salt” mined from the ground or separated from ocean water by evaporation (aka “rock salt”). Throughout the article in question, definitions are mixed up consistently- most clearly in the quote “Salt is a vital substance for the survival and health of all living creatures” where use of the chemical definition isn’t specified, and the common definition is implied.

Now each relevant point from the article will be addressed in order:

“Animals have instinctively forged trails to natural salt sources to satisfy their need for salt.”

Some animals (mainly herbivores) have been observed to lick naturally occurring rock salt, sometimes as part of their migration patterns. Yet, there are many areas where those animals can’t access rock salt, and many cases where those animals just don’t seek it. Some farmers and farming authorities insist rock salt is required for proper health of livestock, and in most cases, I agree. The typical modern diet for livestock is very poor, and licking salt is one way to offset lack of minerals. Even if the animal has a seemingly optimal diet (like a pasture with plenty of space) the soil may be depleted, and with how much toxins modern livestock are exposed to (via injections, pollution, etc), an additional source of minerals could very well be beneficial. Although (for reasons explained later) living food is the optimal source of any nutrient found in rock salt. Many farmers have picked up on the fact that the healthier their animals- the less likely they are to lick salt. Wild animals often go through periods where food is scarce due to winter, competition, etc, so it’s very possible herbivores only seek salt when they aren’t able to get those minerals via plants, but there’s also the possibility that they have done this for so long their bodies evolved to make use of licking salt on occasion for a unique function.

Salt (and sodium compounds) can absolutely cause dehydration. The blood has a very tightly controlled concentration of sodium compounds, the kidneys regulate this, keeping it within optimal range. When more sodium compounds are absorbed into the blood making the concentration beyond optimal range, kidneys remove the excess, along with water and nutrients (unavoidably- as the kidneys have such a complex job they can’t just remove one compound alone).
